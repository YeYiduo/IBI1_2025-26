This practical was really fun and easier to follow than I expected. I started by building a basic 
stochastic SIR model, and I was surprised to see that the results looked different every time I ran it
 because it uses random probabilities. It was cool to watch the number of susceptible, infected, and 
recovered people change over time in the plot.
Adding vaccination was my favourite part. I could clearly see how higher vaccination rates lowered
 the infection peak and eventually stopped the disease from spreading. It helped me really understand
 how herd immunity works in real life.
The 2D spatial SIR model was the most challenging part. I had to be careful with the grid edges,
 infecting only 8 neighbours, and plotting multiple heatmaps to show the spread over time. Once I 
fixed the code, it was really satisfying to see the disease spread and then fade out across the map.
Overall, I learned a lot about using Python to model real‑world problems. I got more comfortable 
with numpy loops and plotting graphs. This practical made computational biology feel practical 
and easy to understand.
